# Utility Problem

The utility problem is the gap between high performance on common AI evaluations and large, durable real-world usefulness. A system can ace exams, coding contests, or game benchmarks while still failing to create much economic or operational value if those evaluations omit the interactive, sequential, and environment-specific structure of real work.

## Why It Matters

- Strong benchmark results can overstate how ready a system is for deployment.
- Teams may optimize for easily measured scores instead of for user outcomes, workflow fit, or persistence across time.
- The problem explains why rapid gains in model capability do not automatically show up as equally large changes in GDP, business processes, or end-user leverage.
- Utility claims can also be overstated when they are extrapolated from eye-catching demos instead of from capabilities that work with near-100% reliability.

## Design Implications

- Evaluation should include human interaction when the real task depends on back-and-forth clarification or coordination.
- Sequential task setups are often better than i.i.d. batches because useful agents should accumulate familiarity and context.
- Metrics should connect to task utility, not only to abstract correctness on a decontextualized benchmark.
- Teams should separate aspirational capabilities from reliable capabilities before inferring economic impact or product readiness.
