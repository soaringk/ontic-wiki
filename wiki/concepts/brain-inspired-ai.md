# Brain-Inspired AI

Brain-inspired AI uses neuroscience as a source of architectural hypotheses for artificial intelligence, not merely as biological branding. In the current wiki material, the most important claim is that future systems may need more than larger Transformer-style feed-forward computation: they may need richer unit complexity, long-range feedback, parallel perception, and embodied action loops.

## Why It Matters

- Early artificial neural networks borrowed directly from simplified models of biological neurons, but modern deep learning has largely advanced independently of neuroscience.
- The interview argues that current models are strong at System 2 reasoning while leaving much System 1 sensorimotor intelligence untouched.
- Embodied robots and brain-computer interfaces are framed as premature if they lack robust perception, decision, and action loops rather than only impressive mechanical control.
- Neuroscience may contribute not only biological metaphors but also analysis methods for interpretability, ablation, neural manifolds, criticality, and feedback-driven hypothesis generation.

## Architectural Hypotheses

- Biological neurons are more structurally and dynamically complex than the simple units used in most artificial networks.
- Long-range feedback may help with ambiguous, uncertain, or shortcut-seeking problems that pure feed-forward processing handles poorly.
- Parallel perceptual processing matters for open-ended physical environments where serial token processing is too slow or brittle.
- A future general system may need both System 2 reasoning and System 1 perception-action intelligence.
