# Summary

This video interview with Liu Jia, presented under the original Chinese title `清华教授：应试教育在AI面前全军覆没，我们应该如何重新学习？`, argues that AI changes both the meaning of intelligence and the purpose of education. The transcript is raw ASR, so exact wording should be treated cautiously, but the main argument is stable: knowledge memorization and exam-oriented training lose value when models can supply knowledge on demand, while human learning should shift toward self-knowledge, first-principles reasoning, AI-native collaboration, and zero-to-one creativity.

# Key Claims

- Intelligence has two core components: learning that generalizes beyond memorization, and emergence from sufficient scale plus structural complexity.
- Current large models are strong at System 2 rational work such as mathematics, programming, legal reasoning, and structured knowledge tasks, but they still lack important System 1 capacities tied to fast perception, embodied action, and survival-oriented sensorimotor intelligence.
- The next major AI frontier may require a renewed brain-science influence, especially richer neural complexity, long-range feedback, parallel processing, and embodied perception-action loops.
- The interview distinguishes low-level subjective experience from high-level self-consciousness, and proposes death awareness and meaning-seeking as a human-specific driver that AI may not share.
- Exam-oriented education is framed as training for closed datasets; AI-era learning should instead prepare people for open-ended problems where interests, goals, and logical starting points matter more than stored facts.
- AI-native thinking is not merely using chatbots or prompt tricks; it is a deeper shift toward cooperating with AI as an active partner and integrating it into one's cognitive workflow.

# Why It Matters

The interview connects several existing wiki themes: the move from benchmark-like closed tasks to open-ended utility, the need for grounded and embodied agents, and the pressure AI places on human education. Its education claim is especially practical: if knowledge access becomes cheap, durable advantage shifts toward asking better questions, finding logical origins, choosing meaningful goals, and building with AI rather than competing with it on memorized facts.

# Open Questions

- The source makes strong claims about education becoming obsolete; the narrower durable claim is that memorization-heavy, closed-dataset education is less valuable, not that all schooling, mentorship, social learning, or credentialing has already lost value.
- The argument that AI lacks death awareness is philosophically contestable and depends on how future digital continuity, persistence, and self-modeling are implemented.
- The claim that current models have largely solved System 2 work should be checked against reliability standards, not only impressive demonstrations.
- The proposed brain-science path is directional rather than operational: it identifies missing ingredients such as feedback and parallel perception, but does not yet specify a validated architecture.
