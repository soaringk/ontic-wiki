# Summary

This essay argues that the durable pattern across AI history is that general methods which scale with computation outperform approaches that hard-code human domain knowledge. Search and learning matter because they keep improving as compute grows, while built-in human understanding often helps only temporarily and can block long-run progress.

# Key Claims

- The most important long-run AI advances come from general-purpose methods that exploit increasing computation rather than from hand-crafted domain structure.
- Researchers repeatedly overinvest in human knowledge because it gives short-term gains and feels intellectually satisfying.
- Search and learning are the two main classes of methods that seem to scale well with very large amounts of computation.
- The contents of human minds are too complex to encode directly; the right goal is to build meta-methods that discover useful structure rather than to build in our own discoveries.

# Why It Matters

The essay is a standing warning against confusing short-run performance gains with durable research direction. It does not say priors are useless in every form, but it strongly suggests that priors should be learned by scalable general methods rather than designed as fixed human-facing shortcuts.

# Open Questions

- Are large language-model priors best understood as the kind of human-knowledge shortcut this essay warns against, or as a scalable learned prior that remains compatible with the lesson?
- If search and learning are the core scalable meta-methods, how much of today's progress should be credited to algorithmic advances versus data, compute, and productized evaluation loops?
